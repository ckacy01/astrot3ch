TIME LINE

1989-1994

  • (1989) Proposed to investigate a successor telescope to the HST to study high redshift galaxies.
  • (1990) Spherical aberration of the HST halted the development of the NGST.
  • (1993) It was recommended to extend the life of the HST, study a 4m telescope and support research on the origin of galaxies and stars.
  • (1994) The “Hi-Z” telescope with a 4m mirror in an elliptical orbit was proposed.

1995-1996

  • (1995) NASA promoted a lower-cost 8m telescope successor to Hubble.
  • (1996) Designs for an 8m deployable mirror at L2 orbit were studied, with simulations supporting detection of galaxies at redshifts up to 15.

1997-2001

  • (1997-2000) Science goals for NGST were established, revealing budget overruns and launch delays.
  • (August 2000) NASA designated STScI as the Science and Operations Center.
  • (2001) The mirror size was reduced to 6m+. TRW/Ball Aerospace and Lockheed-Martin bid for the contract, and JPL was selected for the Mid-Infrared Instrument (MIRI).

2002

  • (Early 2002) NGST moved to Phase B, transferring responsibilities to the Prime Contractor.
  • (January 2002) NASA selected micro-shutter technology for NIRSpec.
  • (June 2002) Dr. Marcia Rieke’s team was chosen for NIRCAM, and the US science team for MIRI was announced.
  • (September 2002) TRW was selected as the prime contractor, and NGST was renamed the James Webb Space Telescope (JWST).

2003-2004

  • (2003) ESA offered an Ariane 5 launcher, leading to a reduced primary mirror size (25 square meters) and fewer segments (18).
  • (2004) JWST’s launch was rescheduled for August 2011, requiring budget and plan adjustments.
  • (2004) The first beryllium segment blanks were delivered for machining.

2005

  • (2005) Most science instrument teams, except NIRSpec, passed preliminary design reviews and began developing long lead items.
  • (2005) Brush Wellman delivered all 20 beryllium segments for lightweighting.
  • (2005) NASA administrator approved the use of the Ariane 5 rocket, formalizing ESA's contributions.

2006

  • (2006) Technical readiness review for the PDR remained on schedule for January 2007.
  • (2006) All 18 mirror segments were being machined; Technical Readiness Level 6 for major technologies was on track.
  • (2006) Concerns were raised about low funding contingency, but levels were comparable to those of Chandra at the same phase.

2007-2008

  • (2007) NIRSpec and FGS/TFI teams passed their Critical Design Reviews (CDR).
  • (2007) NASA, ESA, and CSA signed memoranda of understanding for JWST development and operations.
  • (2008) The first flight primary mirror segment completed fine-polishing and cryo-testing, validating the mirror polishing process.

2009-2010

  • (2009) Instrument engineering units were being integrated and tested for delivery to GSFC in 2010.
  • (2010) The Sunshield system passed its CDR in January, and the Project passed its MCDR in April.
  • (2010) All instrument engineering units were delivered to GSFC for integration into the ISIM structure.
